The Myth: “Mozart Will Make You Smarter!”

Ever heard that listening to classical music—especially Mozart—will boost your IQ or make you ace your next exam? This idea, known as the “Mozart Effect,” has been music to students’ ears for decades. But is it true?

Why Do Students Love Music While Studying?

Music is everywhere on campus: in libraries, coffee shops, and even during late-night cram sessions. Students often turn to tunes to:

  • Make long study sessions less boring
  • Reduce stress and anxiety
  • Boost motivation and focus

But does your playlist really help you learn, or is it just background noise?

What the Science Really Says

1. Music Can Help—But Not the Way You Think

According to the National University, music can reduce stress and ease test anxiety, which might help you perform better in high-pressure situations. Dr. Masha Godkin notes that music activates both sides of your brain, potentially maximizing learning and memory (National University: Can Music Help You Study and Focus?).

BUT: Music doesn’t magically make you smarter. The benefits are more about mood and motivation than actual brainpower.

2. It’s Personal: One Size Doesn’t Fit All

Everyone responds differently to music. Some students find certain genres—like classical or video game soundtracks—helpful, while others get distracted, especially by songs with lyrics or unfamiliar rhythms (UMGC: How Music Can Help You Study).

  • Binaural beats (special audio tones) at 15Hz have been shown to enhance working memory and focus (JMScheibe: The Scientific Benefits of Listening to Music While Studying).

3. Distraction Is Real

Psychologists from Texas A&M University warn that multitasking is a myth. When you study and listen to music, especially music with lyrics, your brain can’t fully focus on both. This can hurt your ability to remember information (Texas A&M Liberal Arts: Does Listening to Music Really Help You Study?).

“When you’re doing two things at the same time, like studying and listening to music, and one of the things requires cognitive effort, there will be a cost to how much information you can retain.” — Dr. Brian Anderson, Cognitive Psychologist

If you study with music but take exams in silence, you might have trouble recalling what you learned.

What Should You Do?

  • Experiment: Try studying with different types of music (or none at all) and see what works best for you.
  • Avoid Lyrics: Instrumental music or ambient sounds are less likely to distract you.
  • Match Exam Conditions: If your test will be in silence, practice studying in silence too.
  • Listen to Your Brain: If you find yourself singing along instead of solving equations, it’s time to hit pause!

The Bottom Line

Music can make studying more enjoyable and less stressful, but it won’t magically boost your grades. The best study soundtrack is the one that helps you focus—whether that’s Mozart, lo-fi beats, or good old-fashioned silence.
